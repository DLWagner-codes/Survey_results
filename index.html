Key Findings

Four primary takeaways from the three-cohort survey.

64%
Overall endorsement rate for Option 1 ("Cornerstones / Pillars") across all 136 respondents — the clear plurality winner in every cohort.
0%
Gildre and EmpowerHer showed zero endorsement of Options 2 and 3. "Levers/Gears" and "Activators" did not resonate in these communities.
15%
Connectpreneur was the only cohort to endorse Option 2 ("Levers/Gears"), suggesting a mechanical-systems orientation in that community's vocabulary.
~⅓
Of "Other" respondents preferred "Levers" applied to the 12 (not the 4) — signaling conceptual appeal of the term, but disagreement with how Option 2 allocated it.

Analyst Notes & Strategic Implications

  • "Cornerstones and Pillars" has strong cross-community appeal. The architectural metaphor reads as stable, foundational, and credible — well-suited to a framework backed by decades of research and meta-analysis.
  • The word "Levers" carries latent demand. A meaningful subset wants lever-language associated with the 12 behavioral pillars specifically, suggesting "activation" or "operability" is an important psychological signal for that group.
  • Option 3 received zero organic support. The "Activators" term failed to generate endorsement in any cohort and can likely be deprioritized in future naming discussions.
  • Consider a hybrid framing for future testing. A variant like "4 Cornerstones and 12 Levers of Entrepreneurial Behavior" may consolidate support by merging the architectural stability of Option 1 with the operational energy a subset desired.
  • A notable debate emerged in Gildre comments around structural complexity. A subset of respondents questioned whether naming the 12 behavioral dimensions at all adds unnecessary friction. Their argument: lead with the 4 (whatever label is chosen — levers, cornerstones, etc.) and treat the 12 as a subtitle layer — present but subordinate — rather than co-equal branded terms. This is a meaningful UX and communication signal worth exploring in messaging architecture.
  • EmpowerHer's small n (20) limits statistical confidence for that cohort individually. Results trend parallel to Gildre but should be validated with additional outreach before treating as conclusive.
